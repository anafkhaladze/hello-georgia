About Bagrati Cathedral

Best time to visit : ALL SEASON

Bagrati Cathedral, located on Ukimerioni Hill in Kutaisi, was built in 1003 during the reign of Bagrat III. It holds great architectural and symbolic significance as a unifying monument of Georgia. According to Matiane Kartlisai, Bagrat III gathered nobles, clergy, and foreign guests for its consecration, giving it national importance.

An inscription near the north window, the oldest use of Arabic numerals in Georgia, confirms the construction date as 1003. The cathedral remained intact until 1692 when Ottomans seized Kutaisi and blew it up. In 1770, King Solomon I liberated it, though further damage occurred. Today, the fully restored cathedral stands as a historical landmark. Initially listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1994, it was later removed after restoration.

Architecture

Bagrati Cathedral is a triconch cross-domed structure with a high dome on four free-standing piers. The west arm has three naves, with two-story side naves and choirs. The architect introduced a unique fixture system, a novelty in Georgian architecture.

Originally adorned with mosaics, the cathedral features intricately carved gates and a fresco of the Virgin in the south gate. A stone wall enclosed auxiliary buildings and a bell tower. A three-story tower in the northwest, likely the bishop’s residence, predates the cathedral’s completion.

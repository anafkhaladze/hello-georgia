ADJARA

About Adjara

Adjara is historical-geographical site of Georgia, which is located in the gorge of Adjaratskali. The territory of the Adjara region has great history. It was populated from ancient time. Except cultural monuments from different period, there is a wonderful nature in here, like sunny beaches, greenery and very impressive mountainous Adjara. You can meet the local lifestyle and culture in the mountainous villages, which are far from the bustle of modern civilization.

History

Adjara, one of Georgiaâ€™s oldest regions, was historically part of Upper Kartli and Meskheti. First mentioned in the 11th century by Leonti Mroveli, its history dates back to the Stone Age, with Bronze and Iron Age artifacts found in the area. Christianity was introduced here by Apostle Andrew. In the 15th-17th centuries, Adjara was ruled by Gurieli and later conquered by the Ottomans, leading to widespread Islamization while preserving Georgian identity. After Ottoman rule ended in 1878, it became part of the Russian Empire, later an autonomous republic under Soviet rule, a status it retains in independent Georgia.

Culture

Adjara is a culturally rich region in Georgia, known for its unique traditions, folklore, and cuisine. Men wore chokhas with weapons, while women dressed in layered outfits with veils. Folklore features lively dances like "Khorumi" and "Gandagana," celebrated in festivals with music, games, and traditional food. The cuisine is dairy-rich, with dishes like Adjarian Khachapuri and Baklava. The region also has a strong history of viticulture, and beekeeping.

Geography

Adjara, in southwestern Georgia, borders Guria, Samtskhe-Javakheti, and Turkey, with Batumi as its center. It has a humid subtropical climate, with hot summers and mild winters, while mountainous areas are colder and snowier. The region's main river is Adjaristskali, and Mount Kanli is its highest peak. The Kobuleti-Chakvi Ridge divides Adjara into seaside and mountainous zones, shaping its climate.
